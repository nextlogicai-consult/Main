Nobody buys an AI chatbot because they enjoy buying software. They buy one because they believe it will save time, convert more leads, or reduce support costs. The problem is most vendors are very good at selling the belief and very vague about the math.

Let me give you the math.

## The Basic Formula

ROI on an AI chatbot comes down to two things: what it saves you and what it costs you.

What it saves you has three potential components. Time savings from deflecting questions your team currently handles manually. Revenue gains from faster lead response or higher conversion. And error reduction from removing manual steps that currently produce mistakes.

What it costs you is the build or subscription cost, the time spent on setup and training, and the ongoing maintenance.

If the savings exceed the costs over a 12-month period, it is worth doing. If they do not, it is not. This sounds obvious but very few people actually run the numbers before committing.

## Step 1: Quantify Your Current Cost

Start with the time your team spends on the problem the chatbot would solve.

If you are building a support chatbot, count the hours per week your team spends answering repetitive questions. Multiply by your average hourly labour cost. That is your weekly cost. Multiply by 52 for the annual number.

If you are building a lead qualification chatbot, estimate how many leads per month are currently going cold because nobody responds fast enough. Estimate what percentage of those you would convert if response time improved. Multiply by your average deal value.

Write down both numbers. That is your potential upside.

## Step 2: Be Realistic About Deflection Rate

A chatbot will not handle every conversation. A well-built one might deflect 60 to 70 percent of repetitive support queries. A poorly built one might deflect 20 percent before customers give up and call you anyway.

Apply a conservative deflection rate to your time savings number. If you spend 10 hours a week on support questions and the chatbot deflects 60 percent, your real saving is 6 hours a week, not 10.

Use 50 percent as your starting assumption unless you have data that suggests otherwise. It is better to underestimate the benefit and be pleasantly surprised than to overestimate it and feel burned.

## Step 3: Add Up the Real Costs

The build cost or subscription is the easy part. Add to it the time you will spend on setup, writing the initial content, testing, and training your team. For a small business, this is typically 20 to 40 hours of internal time depending on complexity.

Then add ongoing maintenance. Chatbots need to be updated when your offerings change, when new questions emerge, or when you notice it is giving wrong answers. Budget 2 to 4 hours a month for this, at least in the first year.

Price that time at your actual cost, not at zero. Your time has value even when you are not billing it to a client.

## Step 4: Run the 12-Month Calculation

Take your annual savings estimate, apply your conservative deflection rate, and subtract your total costs for the year including setup time.

If the number is positive, the chatbot pays for itself. If it is not, either the problem is not big enough to justify automation, or the tool is too expensive for the scale of your operation.

A few things that often tip the calculation:

Response time improvements are frequently undervalued. If your chatbot responds to a lead in 30 seconds instead of 4 hours, the conversion rate improvement alone can justify the entire build cost.

Consistency is also undervalued. A chatbot gives the same answer every time. If your current process involves different team members giving different answers to the same question, the error cost is real even if it is hard to quantify precisely.

## What to Do With This

Run this calculation before you talk to any vendor. Walk into that conversation knowing your numbers, knowing what a realistic deflection rate looks like, and knowing what the break-even point is for your specific situation.

If the vendor cannot help you stress-test those numbers, that tells you something useful about how they work.

If you have spent any time looking into automating your business workflows, you have almost certainly run into both Make and Zapier. They both connect your apps. They both run automations. They both claim to be the easy option.

But they are genuinely different tools and the right one for you depends on what you are trying to do and how comfortable you are with a bit of complexity.

Here is the honest comparison.

## What They Both Do

Both Make (formerly Integromat) and Zapier are no-code automation platforms. You connect apps, set up triggers and actions, and the platform runs the workflow automatically when the trigger fires.

The classic example: when a new lead fills out your contact form, automatically add them to your CRM, send them a welcome email, and notify your team in Slack. Neither Make nor Zapier requires you to write code to do this.

That is where the similarity mostly ends.

## Zapier: The Simpler Starting Point

Zapier is built for people who want to get something working fast without thinking too hard about it.

The interface is straightforward. You pick a trigger app, pick an action app, map the fields, and turn it on. For simple one-to-one automations, you can be up and running in 15 minutes with no prior experience.

The app library is enormous. Zapier connects with over 6,000 apps, which means whatever tools you use, there is a good chance Zapier already has a connector for them.

The downside is that Zapier gets expensive quickly. The free plan is limited and once you start running meaningful volume or need more complex logic, the pricing jumps significantly. Multi-step automations, filters, and anything beyond basic triggers and actions require a paid plan.

Zapier is also less flexible when workflows get complicated. If you need to loop through a list of items, do conditional logic across multiple branches, or handle errors gracefully, Zapier can feel limiting.

## Make: More Power, More Learning Curve

Make uses a visual canvas where you drag and drop modules and connect them with lines. It looks like a flowchart, which is either intuitive or confusing depending on how your brain works.

The learning curve is steeper. Your first Make scenario will take longer to build than your first Zap. But once you understand how it works, Make can handle automations that would be impossible or prohibitively expensive in Zapier.

Make is significantly cheaper for the same volume of automation. The pricing is based on operations rather than tasks, and the free tier is more generous. For small businesses running meaningful automation volume, Make is often a third to half the cost of Zapier.

Make also handles complexity better. Looping through arrays, error handling, branching logic, data transformation. If your workflow has conditions, Make handles them more elegantly.

The app library is smaller than Zapier's but covers most common business tools. If you use niche software, check Make's connector list before committing.

## Which One Should You Start With

If you are new to automation and want to get something working this week without a learning investment, start with Zapier. The simplicity is real and the time to first working automation is lower.

If you are comfortable spending a few hours learning a tool in exchange for more flexibility and lower ongoing cost, Make is worth the investment. Most people who switch from Zapier to Make do not go back.

If your automations involve AI steps, Make has better native AI integration and more flexibility around how AI fits into a workflow.

If budget is a significant concern, Make is almost always the more economical choice at scale.

## The Practical Answer

For most small businesses we work with, we start with whichever platform they are already using or have heard of. Getting something working is more important than getting the perfect tool.

If you are starting from scratch and your workflows are simple, try Zapier's free tier. If you hit a wall with pricing or flexibility within a few months, migrate to Make. The concepts transfer and Make has import tools that make migration manageable.

Neither platform requires a developer to use for standard business workflows. Both have good documentation and active communities. The best one is the one you will actually use.

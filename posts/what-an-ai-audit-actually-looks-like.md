The word audit has a way of making people nervous. It conjures images of accountants and clipboards and someone finding something wrong.

An AI audit is nothing like that. Here is exactly what it is, what we look at, and what you walk away with.

## What Triggers an Audit

Most clients come to us with one of three situations.

The first is curiosity. They have heard enough about AI that they feel like they should be doing something with it, but they have no idea where to start and do not want to waste money figuring it out.

The second is a specific pain point. Something in their operation is costing them time or money and they suspect AI might be able to help, but they are not sure if that is true or how to approach it.

The third is a bad experience. They tried an AI tool, it did not deliver what was promised, and now they want a second opinion on whether AI is actually viable for their business.

All three are good reasons to do an audit. The process is the same regardless of where you are starting from.

## What We Actually Look At

An audit has three components.

The first is your current workflow. We look at how work actually moves through your business, not how you think it does or how you wish it did. We talk to the people doing the work, not just the people managing it. There is almost always a gap between the two.

We are looking for repetition, manual handoffs between systems, and any place where a human is doing something that follows a consistent enough pattern that a machine could handle it.

The second is your data. AI is only as useful as the information it has access to. We look at where your data lives, how clean it is, and whether it is in a format that can actually be used. A lot of businesses have valuable data trapped in PDFs, email threads, or spreadsheets that nobody has structured properly. Sometimes the most important part of an audit is identifying that and figuring out what to do about it.

The third is your tools. We look at what software you are already paying for, whether you are using it fully, and where the gaps are. In many cases, the tools you already have can do more than you realize. Before we recommend buying anything new, we want to know what you already own.

## What You Get at the End

The output of an audit is a written report. It includes a summary of what we found, a prioritized list of opportunities ranked by impact and effort, and a plain-language recommendation for where to start.

We also include a honest section on what is not worth doing. If something looked promising but the data is not there to support it, or the ROI does not justify the build cost, we say so. The goal is not to sell you a project. The goal is to tell you the truth about where AI can actually help.

Most audits take about two weeks from the initial conversation to the final report. The first session is a 90-minute working session with you and anyone else on your team who touches the workflows we are examining. After that, most of the work is on our side.

## What Happens After

Some clients take the report and implement the recommendations themselves. That is a completely valid outcome and we write the report with that in mind.

Others want us to build what we recommended. In that case, the audit becomes the foundation for the project scope, which saves time and usually results in a better build because we are not guessing about the requirements.

Either way, the audit pays for itself. Even if you never work with us again after receiving it, knowing where AI can genuinely help your business and where it cannot is worth more than whatever you spent finding out.

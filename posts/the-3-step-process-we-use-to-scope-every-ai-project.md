Most AI projects fail before they start. Not because the technology does not work, but because nobody took the time to figure out what problem they were actually solving.

We have a process for this. It is not complicated. But it is the reason our implementations tend to stick when a lot of others do not.

Here is exactly what we do, in order, before we recommend anything.

## Step 1: Map the Time

The first thing we do with any new client is a simple time audit. We ask them to write down every recurring task their team does in a given week, roughly how long each one takes, and how often it happens.

This sounds basic. Most people have never actually done it.

What usually comes out of this exercise surprises people. The tasks that feel like they take forever often do not. And the tasks that feel minor, things like copying data between systems, sending follow-up emails, updating spreadsheets, add up to a staggering amount of time when you multiply them across a week.

We are looking for three things in this map. Tasks that are high frequency. Tasks that are low complexity. And tasks that follow a consistent pattern every time they are done. Those three criteria together are the clearest signal that something can be automated.

## Step 2: Identify the Constraint

Once we have the time map, we do not just go after the biggest number. We look for the constraint.

The constraint is the task that, if you removed it or dramatically reduced it, would have a cascading positive effect on everything else. Sometimes that is the most time-consuming task. Often it is not.

A useful question here is: what is the thing your best people spend time on that a less skilled person, or a well-designed system, could handle just as well? That gap between the skill required and the skill being applied is usually where the most valuable automation lives.

We also look at where errors happen. Manual processes that touch multiple systems, involve copy-pasting, or depend on someone remembering to do something are prime candidates. Not just because of the time they take, but because of the cost when they go wrong.

## Step 3: Define What Done Looks Like

This is the step most people skip and the reason most AI projects drift.

Before we build anything, we write down in plain language what success looks like. Not in vague terms like "save time" or "improve efficiency." In specific, measurable terms.

Something like: the follow-up email goes out within two hours of a job being marked complete, without anyone having to do it manually, and the open rate stays above 40 percent.

That is a definition of done you can actually test against. It tells you what to build, how to know if it is working, and when to stop tweaking and call it finished.

This document, what we call the scope brief, is what both sides sign off on before anything gets built. It protects the client from scope creep and it protects us from building the wrong thing.

## Why This Order Matters

You might be wondering why we do not just ask clients what they want and build that.

We tried that early on. It does not work well.

What clients say they want and what they actually need are often different things. Not because they are wrong, but because they are too close to their own operation to see it clearly. The three-step process creates the distance needed to make a better decision.

It also sets realistic expectations. When someone has gone through a proper scoping process, they understand why we are building what we are building. That understanding makes training easier, adoption higher, and the post-launch relationship smoother.

If you want to run this process yourself before booking a call, go for it. The time map alone is worth doing. Most of our clients tell us it is the most useful 90 minutes they spent in a given month.
